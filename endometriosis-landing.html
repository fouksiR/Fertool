Endometriosis and Fertility Preservation

Endometriosis is associated with subfertility in 30-50% of affected women. Both the disease process and surgical interventions can progressively reduce ovarian reserve.

Oocyte cryopreservation provides an opportunity to preserve reproductive potential before further ovarian reserve decline. This is particularly relevant for women planning surgery or those concerned about age-related and disease-related fertility decline.

30-50% of women with endometriosis experience subfertility
83-85% oocyte survival rate after vitrification

How Endometriosis Affects Fertility

Endometriosis impacts reproductive potential through multiple mechanisms:

  • Endometriomas cause direct follicular damage and reduce functional ovarian tissue
  • Chronic peritoneal inflammation creates a hostile environment for gametes and embryos
  • Pelvic adhesions distort tubo-ovarian anatomy, impairing natural conception
  • Surgical excision, while often necessary, results in additional follicle loss

Ovarian Reserve Considerations

Women with ovarian endometriomas demonstrate:

  • Reduced anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH) levels compared to age-matched controls
  • Accelerated decline in ovarian reserve markers (0.3 ng/mL/year vs 0.1 ng/mL/year)
  • Lower antral follicle counts on transvaginal ultrasound
Clinical consideration

Significant ovarian reserve compromise occurs from the disease itself, independent of surgical intervention. Fertility preservation prior to surgery may optimize outcomes.

The clinical dilemma: surgical management may improve natural fertility but unavoidably reduces follicle reserve. This forms the rationale for considering oocyte cryopreservation before surgical intervention.

Oocyte Cryopreservation Protocol

Typical stimulation cycle: 10-14 days

Protocol details

GnRH antagonist protocols minimize cycle length and OHSS risk. Serial monitoring (ultrasound and estradiol) guides optimal trigger timing for oocyte maturity.

Expected cycle requirements

Most women with endometriosis require 2-3 stimulation cycles to achieve 10-20 mature oocytes, the target number associated with favorable live birth rates.

Evidence Base

Recent literature on oocyte cryopreservation outcomes in women with endometriosis:

Comparable outcomes with adequate oocyte numbers

When similar numbers of mature oocytes are vitrified, women with endometriosis achieve live birth rates comparable to those undergoing elective fertility preservation. The primary impact is quantitative (reduced yield per cycle) rather than qualitative (oocyte competence).

Key Outcome Data (La Marca et al. 2025)

  • Post-warming oocyte survival: 83-85%
  • Cumulative live birth rate: 46.4% (age-dependent)
  • Embryo quality metrics: comparable to age-matched controls without endometriosis
  • Early pregnancy loss rates: similar to age-matched controls

Impact of Prior Surgery

Previous ovarian surgery for endometriomas consistently reduces oocyte yield:

  • No prior surgery: 9.4 ± 0.6 mature oocytes per cycle (mean ± SEM)
  • Prior ovarian surgery: 30-35% reduction in oocytes retrieved
  • Multiple surgeries: cumulative progressive decline
Clinical implications

Women with previous ovarian surgery typically require additional stimulation cycles (3-4 vs 2-3) to achieve target oocyte numbers. Successful outcomes remain achievable with appropriate cycle planning.

Safety Profile

Controlled ovarian stimulation for fertility preservation:

  • Does not accelerate endometriosis disease progression
  • Does not exacerbate symptoms during or after treatment
  • No evidence of increased recurrence rates post-stimulation

Clinical Pathway

1. Ovarian Reserve Assessment

Initial evaluation should include:

  • Serum anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH)
  • Transvaginal ultrasound antral follicle count (AFC)
  • Early follicular phase FSH and estradiol (day 2-5)

2. Reproductive Endocrinology Consultation

Referral to specialist experienced in endometriosis-related infertility. Discussion points:

  • Expected oocyte yield based on current reserve markers and surgical history
  • Recommended number of stimulation cycles to achieve target oocyte number
  • Optimal timing relative to planned surgical intervention
  • Predicted cumulative live birth rate based on age and anticipated yield

3. Multidisciplinary Coordination

Communication between gynecology and reproductive endocrinology regarding:

  • Surgical approach and timing considerations
  • Current medical management and symptom control
  • Disease stage and progression risk
  • Integrated fertility preservation and treatment strategy

Financial Considerations

Oocyte cryopreservation involves substantial out-of-pocket costs in most jurisdictions:

  • Stimulation cycle: $10,000-$15,000 AUD (including medications)
  • Annual storage fees: $500-$1,000 AUD
  • Multiple cycles: typically 2-4 cycles required
  • Future warming and IVF: $5,000-$10,000 AUD per attempt
Insurance coverage

Some private health insurance policies provide partial coverage for medically indicated fertility preservation. Patients should verify benefits and explore available payment options during initial consultation.

Summary

Oocyte cryopreservation is feasible and effective for women with endometriosis

Outcomes are primarily determined by maternal age and ovarian reserve at cryopreservation

Multiple stimulation cycles typically required; excellent outcomes achievable with adequate planning

Optimal results achieved before age 35 and before multiple ovarian surgeries

Individualized approach requiring specialist multidisciplinary input
