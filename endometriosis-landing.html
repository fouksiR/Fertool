Why This Matters

Endometriosis affects fertility in up to 50% of patients. The condition can reduce ovarian reserve over time, both from the disease itself and from surgeries that may be needed.

Egg freezing offers a way to preserve fertility options before further decline occurs. It's particularly relevant if you're planning surgery or want to protect against age-related and disease-related changes.

30-50% of endometriosis patients face fertility challenges
83-85% survival rate of frozen eggs when thawed
This guide provides education, not medical advice

Every situation is unique. Use this information to have informed conversations with your specialists about what's right for you.

How Endometriosis Affects Fertility

The condition impacts fertility through several pathways:

  • Ovarian cysts (endometriomas) damage surrounding healthy tissue
  • Chronic inflammation affects the pelvic environment
  • Adhesions can distort anatomy
  • Surgery to treat the disease can further reduce ovarian reserve

The Ovarian Reserve Challenge

Women with endometriomas typically show:

  • Lower AMH levels (ovarian reserve marker)
  • Faster decline in reserve over time
  • Reduced follicle counts on ultrasound
Important finding

Much of the ovarian damage occurs before surgery. This is why timing preservation before surgery can be strategic.

The challenge: surgery may help fertility but risks further reducing reserve. This creates the case for considering egg freezing before intervention.

What Egg Freezing Involves

The process takes about 2 weeks per cycle:

Stimulation (10-14 days)

Daily hormone injections mature multiple eggs. Specialists use protocols proven safe for endometriosis patients.

Monitoring (3-4 visits)

Ultrasounds and blood tests track development to optimize timing.

Retrieval (30 minutes)

Minor procedure under light sedation to collect eggs. Same-day recovery at home.

Vitrification (immediate)

Rapid freezing technique preserves eggs until you're ready to use them.

Multiple cycles are typical

Most patients need 2-3 cycles to collect 10-20 eggs (optimal for good outcomes). This is due to reduced reserve, not egg quality issues.

9-10 eggs per cycle average

What the Research Shows

Recent studies on egg freezing for endometriosis patients reveal:

Success is comparable when adequate eggs are frozen

With similar numbers of eggs, endometriosis patients achieve live birth rates matching those freezing for other reasons. The impact is quantitative (fewer eggs per cycle) rather than qualitative (egg quality).

Key Findings

  • Egg survival rate: 83-85% after thawing
  • Live birth rate: 46% overall (strongly age-dependent)
  • Embryo quality: comparable to non-endometriosis patients
  • Miscarriage rates: similar to age-matched controls

Previous Surgery Impact

Surgery consistently reduces eggs retrieved per cycle:

  • Without prior surgery: average 9-10 mature eggs per cycle
  • With previous surgery: significantly fewer eggs retrieved
  • Multiple surgeries: progressive decline in outcomes
Practical implication

With prior surgery, expect to need more cycles (typically 3-4 instead of 2-3) to reach optimal egg numbers. Success is still achievable with appropriate planning.

Safety Considerations

Ovarian stimulation for egg freezing does not:

  • Cause endometriosis to progress
  • Worsen symptoms
  • Increase disease recurrence risk

Moving Forward

1. Get Ovarian Reserve Testing

Essential baseline before any decision:

  • AMH blood test (ovarian reserve marker)
  • Antral follicle count ultrasound
  • Day 3 FSH and estradiol

2. Consult a Fertility Specialist

Seek a reproductive endocrinologist experienced with endometriosis. Key questions:

  • "Based on my testing, how many eggs per cycle would you expect?"
  • "How many cycles would you recommend?"
  • "Should I freeze before or after planned surgery?"
  • "What's my predicted success rate with X eggs frozen?"

3. Coordinate With Your Gynecologist

Your specialists should communicate about:

  • Surgery timing and type
  • Current symptom management
  • Disease progression and staging
  • Overall fertility strategy

Financial Planning

Egg freezing involves significant costs:

  • Per cycle: $10,000-$15,000 including medications
  • Storage: $500-$1,000 annually
  • Multiple cycles: budget for 2-4 cycles
  • Future use: IVF cycle costs $5,000-$10,000
Insurance coverage varies

Some plans cover fertility preservation for medical conditions like endometriosis. Check benefits and ask about payment plans during consultations.

Key Takeaways

Egg freezing is feasible and effective for endometriosis patients

Success depends primarily on age and reserve at time of freezing

Multiple cycles typically needed but outcomes can be excellent

Best results before age 35 and before multiple surgeries

Work with specialists to create your personalized plan
