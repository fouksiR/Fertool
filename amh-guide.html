Anti-Müllerian Hormone (AMH)

Understanding your ovarian reserve

AMH is produced by follicles in your ovaries. Your AMH level reflects how many eggs remain in your ovaries—called your ovarian reserve. Like egg count, AMH naturally declines with age.

What AMH tells you

  • Your approximate egg supply
  • Whether you might reach menopause earlier than average
  • How many eggs might be retrieved in an egg freezing cycle

This information is particularly valuable if you've had ovarian surgery, chemotherapy, radiation, or have only one ovary.

What AMH doesn't tell you

  • Your egg quality (no test can measure this)
  • Your actual chances of conceiving
  • How quickly your egg count is declining
  • Whether other fertility factors are present

Understanding your result

AMH should always be considered alongside your age. Normal AMH for your age doesn't guarantee fertility, as it only reflects quantity, not quality.

High AMH may indicate polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS), which can actually reduce fertility. PCOS is diagnosed through symptoms (irregular periods, excess male hormones) and ultrasound.

Low AMH suggests lower ovarian reserve. You can still conceive naturally, but you may reach menopause earlier. If children are in your future plans, consider discussing timing with your doctor.

Important note

PCOS can mask low AMH, producing misleadingly normal results.

Clinical Context

AMH testing is most useful when:

  • Planning the timing of fertility preservation
  • Assessing likelihood of ovarian response to stimulation
  • Evaluating women with suspected premature ovarian insufficiency
  • Monitoring ovarian reserve after gonadotoxic treatment
For healthcare providers

AMH values should be interpreted in conjunction with age, antral follicle count, and clinical context. Isolated AMH values have limited predictive value for natural fertility in regularly cycling women.
