Endometriosis and Reproduction

Understanding how endometriosis affects fertility

Explore the Anatomy

Hover over or click different anatomical areas to learn about how endometriosis can affect each location and its impact on fertility.

Uterus
Endometrial & Myometrial Involvement
The uterus can be affected by endometriosis both on its outer surface and within the muscle wall itself.

Common Lesions:

  • Adenomyosis
    Endometrial tissue grows into the uterine muscle wall (myometrium), causing painful periods and heavy bleeding
  • Superficial Lesions
    Endometrial tissue on the outer uterine surface (serosa)
Ovaries
Ovarian Endometriosis
Ovarian involvement is particularly significant for fertility as it directly affects egg quality and quantity. Endometriomas can damage healthy ovarian tissue.

Common Lesions:

  • Endometrioma (Chocolate Cyst)
    Cysts filled with old blood, appearing dark brown. Can damage surrounding ovarian tissue and reduce ovarian reserve
  • Superficial Implants
    Small patches of endometrial tissue on the ovarian surface
  • Adhesions
    Scar tissue that can distort ovarian position and function
Fallopian Tubes
Tubal Involvement
Endometriosis affecting the fallopian tubes can block or damage these critical pathways for egg transport and fertilization.

Common Conditions:

  • Hydrosalpinx
    Tube becomes blocked and fills with clear fluid, preventing egg transport
  • Hematosalpinx
    Tube fills with blood due to endometrial implants bleeding within the tube
  • Peritubal Adhesions
    Scar tissue around tubes affecting their mobility and egg pickup
Cervix & Vagina
Lower Reproductive Tract
Though less common, endometriosis can affect the cervix and vagina, potentially causing pain and bleeding.

Possible Lesions:

  • Cervical Endometriosis
    Implants on the cervix, may cause bleeding after intercourse
  • Vaginal Endometriosis
    Deep nodules in the vaginal wall, can cause deep dyspareunia
Pelvic Cavity
Peritoneal & Deep Endometriosis
The pelvic cavity, including the cul-de-sac (pouch of Douglas), is one of the most common sites for endometriosis. Deep infiltrating disease here can be particularly problematic.

Common Lesions:

  • Superficial Peritoneal Lesions
    Red, white, or brown spots on the peritoneal lining. Can cause inflammation affecting fertility
  • Deep Infiltrating Endometriosis (DIE)
    Lesions penetrating >5mm into tissue, often in cul-de-sac, uterosacral ligaments, or bowel
  • Obliterated Cul-de-sac
    Severe adhesions fill and distort the space behind the uterus
